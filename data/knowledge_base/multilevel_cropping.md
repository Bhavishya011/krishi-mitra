# Multilevel Cropping — Layered Farming Systems
# =================================================
# Source: ICAR / ZBNF / Traditional Indian polyculture practices
# Region focus: North India


## What is Multilevel Cropping?

Multilevel cropping (also called multi-tier cropping, multi-storeyed cropping, or layered farming) is the practice of growing multiple crops of different heights simultaneously on the same piece of land. The crops occupy different vertical layers — just like a natural forest has canopy trees, mid-level shrubs, ground-cover plants, and root systems at different depths.

**Why it works:**
- Different crops capture sunlight at different heights — nothing is wasted
- Different root depths access nutrients and water from different soil layers
- Legumes in the system fix atmospheric nitrogen and share with companion crops
- Ground-cover layer suppresses weeds and retains moisture (natural mulching)
- Pest pressure is reduced because monoculture conditions favor pest buildup
- Total income per acre increases by 40-80% compared to single crop

**The 4 layers:**
1. **Canopy Layer (Oonchi Fasal)** — Tall crop that forms the upper canopy (3-6 feet). Example: Maize, Sorghum, Sugarcane, or fruit trees.
2. **Sub-Canopy Layer (Beech ki Fasal)** — Medium-height crop (2-3 feet) that grows well in partial shade. Example: Arhar/Pigeon pea, Okra, Brinjal.
3. **Ground Cover Layer (Neechi Fasal)** — Low-growing or spreading crop (under 1 foot) that covers the soil. Example: Moong, Cowpea, Pumpkin, Watermelon.
4. **Root/Underground Layer (Zameen ke Andar)** — Root or tuber crops that grow below the soil surface. Example: Radish, Carrot, Turmeric, Ginger.


## Example 1: Winter Season (Rabi) — Wheat + Mustard + Vegetables

**Best for:** Haryana, Punjab, Western UP — October/November to March/April

### Layer Configuration:
- **Canopy:** Mustard (Sarson) — grows to 4-5 feet, spaced in rows 60cm apart
- **Sub-Canopy:** Wheat (Gehun) — grows to 3 feet, sown in between mustard rows (30cm row spacing)
- **Ground Cover:** Palak (Spinach) or Methi (Fenugreek) — sown on borders and edges, harvested within 40-45 days
- **Root Layer:** Radish (Mooli) — sown in alternate rows, harvested within 45-50 days before it competes with wheat

### How it works:
1. Sow mustard first in wide rows (60cm apart).
2. After 15 days, sow wheat between the mustard rows.
3. Sow palak/methi on field borders and headlands.
4. Sow mooli in every 4th or 5th row.
5. Harvest mooli and palak first (45 days) — these are quick cash crops.
6. Mustard flowers attract beneficial pollinators and predators for wheat protection.
7. Harvest mustard (130-140 days) and wheat (140-150 days) separately.

### Expected Returns:
- Wheat alone: ₹40,000-50,000 per acre
- This system: ₹65,000-80,000 per acre (vegetables + mustard oil + wheat grain)
- No additional irrigation needed over what wheat requires alone.

## Example 2: Monsoon Season (Kharif) — Sugarcane + Legumes + Vegetables

**Best for:** Western UP, Haryana, Punjab — February planting (spring sugarcane)

### Layer Configuration:
- **Canopy:** Sugarcane (Ganna) — grows to 8-12 feet, planted in paired rows (30cm-30cm with 120cm gap between pairs)
- **Sub-Canopy:** Arhar / Pigeon Pea (Tur Dal) — planted in the 120cm gap between sugarcane pairs, grows to 4-5 feet
- **Ground Cover:** Moong (Green Gram) — sown in the same gap between sugarcane rows, harvested in 60-65 days before arhar grows tall
- **Root Layer:** Haldi (Turmeric) — planted between sugarcane stools, thrives in partial shade

### How it works:
1. Plant sugarcane in paired rows (February-March).
2. Sow moong between cane pairs immediately — harvested by May-June before monsoon.
3. Plant arhar in May-June when monsoon starts — grows in the gap as sugarcane grows tall.
4. Plant turmeric rhizomes near sugarcane stools in April — partial shade benefits turmeric.
5. Moong fixes nitrogen (~40-60 kg N per acre) reducing fertilizer need for sugarcane.
6. Harvest moong (June), arhar (December), turmeric (January), sugarcane (February next year).

### Expected Returns:
- Sugarcane alone: ₹80,000-1,00,000 per acre
- This system: ₹1,40,000-1,80,000 per acre
- Reduced input costs: moong provides nitrogen, less weeding needed due to ground cover.


## Example 3: Summer Season (Zaid) — Maize + Cowpea + Pumpkin

**Best for:** Haryana, Punjab, Rajasthan — March to June

### Layer Configuration:
- **Canopy:** Maize (Makka) — grows to 6-8 feet, planted at 60cm x 25cm spacing
- **Sub-Canopy:** Cowpea / Lobia — climbs partially on maize stalks, grows to 3-4 feet
- **Ground Cover:** Pumpkin / Kaddu or Watermelon / Tarbooz — vines spread along the ground between maize rows
- **Root Layer:** Ginger (Adrak) — planted near maize rows, benefits from partial shade

### How it works:
1. Sow maize in rows 60cm apart (March).
2. Sow cowpea between maize rows 15 days after maize germination.
3. Plant pumpkin seeds at 2-meter intervals in every alternate inter-row space.
4. Plant ginger rhizomes near maize base (needs shade in summer).
5. Cowpea climbs on maize stalks — no separate staking needed ("Three Sisters" approach adapted for India).
6. Pumpkin leaves shade the ground — natural mulch, conserves moisture.
7. Harvest green maize cobs (70 days), cowpea pods (60-70 days), pumpkin (90-100 days).

### Expected Returns:
- Maize alone: ₹25,000-30,000 per acre
- This system: ₹50,000-65,000 per acre
- Water requirement same as maize alone — pumpkin ground cover reduces evaporation.

### ZBNF Integration:
All three examples benefit enormously from ZBNF practices:
- Apply jeevamrit every 15 days — feeds the diverse soil microbiome that a multilevel system needs
- Use beejamrit for seed treatment of all crops
- The multilevel system itself IS a form of live mulching
- Practice whapasa — the diverse root systems improve soil structure and water infiltration naturally

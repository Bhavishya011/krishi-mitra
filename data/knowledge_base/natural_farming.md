# ========================================
# Source: Subhash Palekar Zero Budget Natural Farming (ZBNF)
# Reference: ICAR-CRIDA / Andhra Pradesh Community Managed Natural Farming (APCNF)


## Jeevamrit — Living Soil Elixir

**What it is:** Jeevamrit is a fermented microbial culture that dramatically boosts soil biology. It introduces and multiplies beneficial microorganisms that unlock nutrients already present in the soil, making them available to plant roots. It is the single most important input in Zero Budget Natural Farming.

**Preparation (for 1 acre):**
1. Take a 200-litre plastic drum (avoid metal).
2. Fill with 200 litres water.
3. Add 10 kg fresh desi cow dung (from indigenous breed, not Jersey/Holstein — desi cow dung contains 300-500 crore beneficial microorganisms per gram).
4. Add 5-10 litres desi cow urine (aged 4-5 days is better).
5. Add 2 kg jaggery (gud) or sugarcane juice — this is food for the microbes.
6. Add 2 kg gram flour (besan) or any pulse flour — provides amino acids for microbial growth.
7. Add a handful of soil from the bund (undisturbed area) of your own farm — this introduces native soil microorganisms already adapted to local conditions.
8. Stir well clockwise with a wooden stick for 2-3 minutes.
9. Cover with a jute sack or cloth (not airtight) and keep in shade.
10. Stir twice daily (morning and evening) for 48 hours.
11. Ready to use after 48 hours. Use within 7 days.

**Application Methods:**
- **Soil Drench:** Mix 200 litres jeevamrit in irrigation water for 1 acre. Apply with every irrigation or at least twice per month.
- **Foliar Spray:** Filter jeevamrit through cloth. Dilute 5-10 litres in 100 litres water. Spray on leaves in morning or evening.

**Benefits:** Increases earthworm population, improves soil structure, suppresses soil-borne diseases, enhances nutrient uptake, reduces water requirement by improving soil water-holding capacity.

**Frequency:** Every 15 days throughout the crop season. For perennial crops, apply monthly.


## Beejamrit — Seed Treatment

**What it is:** Beejamrit is a natural seed treatment that protects seeds and seedlings from soil-borne fungal diseases during germination and early growth. It replaces chemical seed treatments like Thiram, Captan, or Carbendazim.

**Preparation (for treating seeds needed for 1 acre):**
1. Take 20 litres water in a container.
2. Add 5 kg fresh desi cow dung.
3. Add 5 litres desi cow urine.
4. Add 50 grams lime (calcium hydroxide / chuna).
5. Add a fistful of soil from undisturbed farm bund.
6. Mix thoroughly and keep overnight (12 hours).
7. Filter the liquid through cloth.

**Application:**
- Soak seeds in filtered beejamrit for 20 minutes before sowing.
- For fine seeds (like paddy nursery), mix beejamrit with seeds and spread in shade to dry before sowing.
- For sugarcane setts or turmeric rhizomes, dip for 30 minutes.

**Benefits:** Prevents damping-off, root rot, and seedling blight. The lime creates a protective alkaline coating. Cow urine acts as fungicide. Cow dung introduces beneficial Trichoderma and Pseudomonas.


## Mulching — Soil Cover Management

**What it is:** Mulching means covering the soil surface with organic materials to mimic the forest floor. In ZBNF, mulching is considered the third essential practice. Bare soil is exposed to sun, wind, and rain — leading to moisture loss, erosion, and death of surface-dwelling beneficial organisms.

**Types in ZBNF:**

### Soil Mulching (Straw / Residue Mulch)
- **Materials:** Paddy straw, wheat straw, dry leaves, sugarcane trash, groundnut shells, coconut coir — any locally available dry organic matter.
- **Application:** Spread 4-6 inches thick layer on soil surface between crop rows after the crop is 30-45 days old.
- **Benefits:**
  - Conserves soil moisture (reduces irrigation need by 30-50%)
  - Suppresses weed growth (reduces hand-weeding labor)
  - Maintains stable soil temperature (protects roots from extreme heat/cold)
  - Provides habitat for earthworms and beneficial insects
  - Gradually decomposes and adds organic matter to soil

### Live Mulching (Cover Crops)
- **Method:** Grow low-growing cover crops between rows of the main crop.
- **Examples:** Moong (green gram), lobia (cowpea), or dhaincha (Sesbania) between rows of maize, cotton, or sugarcane.
- **Benefits:** Fixes nitrogen, suppresses weeds, adds biomass when cut and laid as mulch.

### Green Leaf Mulching
- **Method:** Collect green leaves from trees like Subabul (Leucaena), Gliricidia, or Neem. Spread around crop base.
- **Benefits:** Rich in nitrogen, decomposes faster, also acts as pest repellent (especially neem leaves).


## Whapasa — Moisture Management

**What it is:** Whapasa is the ZBNF concept of maintaining optimal soil moisture — specifically, a balance of both air and water molecules in the soil. The idea is that roots need both water AND air to function properly. Waterlogged soil is as harmful as bone-dry soil.

**Principle:** Irrigate only enough to maintain whapasa — the condition where the soil has a film of moisture around each soil particle, but air spaces remain open between particles. The soil should feel moist but not wet when squeezed.

**Practices to Achieve Whapasa:**
1. **Reduce irrigation frequency but increase mulching.** A well-mulched field retains moisture 3-5 times longer than bare soil.
2. **Avoid flooding.** Use furrow or drip irrigation instead of flood irrigation. Even for paddy, ZBNF recommends alternate wetting and drying (AWD) rather than continuous submergence.
3. **Build soil organic matter.** Each 1% increase in soil organic matter enables the soil to hold an additional 20,000 litres of water per acre.
4. **Maintain earthworm population.** Earthworm burrows create natural drainage channels and aeration paths. Jeevamrit application dramatically increases earthworm count.

**Indicator:** Dig 6 inches into the soil. If the soil crumbles easily but you can feel moisture on your fingers, whapasa is achieved. If it sticks together in a ball, it is too wet. If it is dusty and falls apart, it needs irrigation.

**Water Savings:** Fields managed with whapasa principle typically require 40-60% less irrigation water compared to conventional flood-irrigated fields, while producing equal or higher yields.
